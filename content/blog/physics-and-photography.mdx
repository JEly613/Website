Photography and physics are more connected than you might think. Understanding how light behaves can dramatically improve your ability to capture compelling images.

## The Inverse Square Law

One of the most important concepts in photography is the inverse square law. As you move a light source away from your subject, the intensity of light decreases proportionally to the square of the distance.

This means doubling the distance reduces the light intensity to one-quarter. Understanding this helps you predict how your lighting will look before you even take the shot.

## Diffraction and Aperture

When you stop down your aperture to f/16 or f/22, you might expect sharper images. But physics has other plans. At very small apertures, diffraction becomes significant, actually reducing sharpness.

The sweet spot for most lenses is around f/8 to f/11, where you balance depth of field with diffraction limits.

## Color Temperature and Black Body Radiation

The "warmth" or "coolness" of light relates directly to black body radiation in physics. A candle flame at 1800K produces warm, orange light, while daylight at 5500K appears neutral, and open shade at 7000K looks cool and blue.

## Putting It Together

You don't need to be a physicist to take great photos, but understanding these principles gives you more control over your craft. Next time you're setting up a shot, think about the physics at play.
