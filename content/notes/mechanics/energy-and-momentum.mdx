## Conservative Forces

A **conservative force** is one that can be written as the derivative of a scalar potential:

$$F = -\frac{dU}{dr}$$

The total work done along a path $p$ is:

$$W = \int_p F \cdot dr = \int_p F_x\, dx$$

Substituting the conservative force definition:

$$W = \int_p -\frac{dU}{dx}\,dx = \int_p -dU$$

For a **closed path**, the starting and ending points are the same, so:

$$\oint_p -dU = 0$$

This is the equivalent definition of a conservative force — one with zero circulation. Both definitions (writable as a potential gradient, and zero circulation) are equivalent. ⋆

## Energy Balance and Conservation of Energy

Starting from the definition of work $W = F\Delta x$ and substituting $\Delta x$ from the kinematics equation $v_f^2 - v_i^2 = 2a\Delta x$ along with $F = ma$:

$$W = ma \cdot \frac{v_f^2 - v_i^2}{2a} = \frac{1}{2}m(v_f^2 - v_i^2)$$

Defining kinetic energy as $K \equiv \frac{1}{2}mv^2$, we have the **Work-Energy Theorem**:

$$\boxed{W = \Delta K}$$

Splitting work into external, internal non-conservative, and internal conservative contributions:

$$W_{\text{ext}} + W_c + W_{nc} = \Delta K$$

Using the definition of potential energy ($W_c = -\Delta U_c$):

$$W_{\text{ext}} + W_{nc} = \Delta K + \Delta U_c$$

When $W_{\text{ext}} = W_{nc} = 0$, energy is conserved. Non-conservative internal work (e.g., friction) becomes thermal energy. The full energy balance equation is:

$$\boxed{W_{\text{ext}} = \Delta K + \Delta U_c + \Delta U_{\text{th}} + \Delta U_{\text{chem}}}$$

From thermodynamics, heat $Q$ is another energy transfer mechanism, giving the most general form:

$$\boxed{Q_{\text{ext}} + W_{\text{ext}} = \Delta K + \Delta U_c + \Delta U_{\text{th}} + \Delta U_{\text{chem}}}$$

## Conservation of Momentum

Momentum is conserved in a system when there is no net external force. Consider two particles interacting via forces $\vec{F}_{12}$ and $\vec{F}_{21}$ over time $\Delta t$:

$$\Delta p_1 = F_{21}\Delta t, \qquad \Delta p_2 = F_{12}\Delta t$$

By Newton's Third Law, $\vec{F}_{21} = -\vec{F}_{12}$, so:

$$\Delta p_1 = -\Delta p_2$$

The total change in momentum is:

$$\sum \Delta p = \Delta p_1 + \Delta p_2 = 0$$

Momentum is conserved as long as there is no net external force on the system. ⋆

## Relative Velocity for Elastic Collisions

For an elastic collision, both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved:

$$m_1 v_{1i} + m_2 v_{2i} = m_1 v_{1f} + m_2 v_{2f}$$

$$\frac{1}{2}m_1 v_{1i}^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_2 v_{2i}^2 = \frac{1}{2}m_1 v_{1f}^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_2 v_{2f}^2$$

The kinetic energy equation often produces a cumbersome quadratic. However, rearranging and dividing the two equations yields a simpler **linear relation**:

$$\boxed{v_{1i} + v_{1f} = v_{2i} + v_{2f}}$$

This can be used together with conservation of momentum to solve for both final velocities algebraically without dealing with a quadratic. ⋆

## Total Kinetic Energy of Any System

The total kinetic energy of any system can be decomposed into center-of-mass motion and internal motion.

Consider a system with total mass $M$ and center of mass at $\mathbf{r}_{\text{cm}}$. For particle $i$ with position $\mathbf{r}_i = \mathbf{r}_{\text{cm}} + \mathbf{r}_{i,\text{cm}}$, we have $\mathbf{v}_i = \mathbf{v}_{\text{cm}} + \mathbf{v}_{i,\text{cm}}$. The kinetic energy is:

$$K_i = \frac{1}{2}m_i v_i^2 = \frac{1}{2}m_i(v_{\text{cm}}^2 + 2\mathbf{v}_{\text{cm}}\cdot\mathbf{v}_{i,\text{cm}} + v_{i,\text{cm}}^2)$$

Summing over all particles, the middle cross term vanishes because $\sum_i m_i \mathbf{r}_{i,\text{cm}} = 0$ by definition of the center of mass. Thus:

$$\boxed{K_{\text{total}} = \frac{1}{2}Mv_{\text{cm}}^2 + \sum_i \frac{1}{2}m_i v_{i,\text{cm}}^2}$$

The total kinetic energy splits neatly into **center-of-mass kinetic energy** and **internal kinetic energy** (motion relative to the center of mass). ⋆

## Total Kinetic Energy of a Two-Body System

For two masses $m_1$ and $m_2$ with velocities $v_1$ and $v_2$, the center-of-mass velocity is:

$$v_{\text{cm}} = \frac{m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2}{m_1 + m_2}$$

The velocity of $m_1$ relative to the center of mass is:

$$v_{1,\text{cm}} = \frac{m_2}{m_1 + m_2}v_{\text{rel}}, \qquad v_{2,\text{cm}} = -\frac{m_1}{m_1 + m_2}v_{\text{rel}}$$

where $v_{\text{rel}} \equiv v_1 - v_2$. Substituting into the internal kinetic energy sum:

$$\sum \frac{1}{2}m_i v_{i,\text{cm}}^2 = \frac{1}{2}\mu v_{\text{rel}}^2$$

where $\mu = \frac{m_1 m_2}{m_1+m_2}$ is the reduced mass. The total kinetic energy of a two-body system is:

$$\boxed{K_{\text{total}} = \frac{1}{2}Mv_{\text{cm}}^2 + \frac{1}{2}\mu v_{\text{rel}}^2}$$

This also gives an easy way to compute energy lost in a perfectly inelastic collision: since $v_{\text{rel}} = 0$ after the collision, the energy lost is exactly $\frac{1}{2}\mu v_{\text{rel}}^2$. ⋆

## Obtaining Equations of Motion Using Energy

For a conservative system depending on a single coordinate $x$, the total energy is:

$$E = \frac{1}{2}m\dot{x}^2 + U(x)$$

Since energy is conserved, $dE/dt = 0$:

$$m\dot{x}\ddot{x} + \frac{dU}{dx}\dot{x} = 0 \implies m\ddot{x} = -\frac{dU}{dx} = F$$

This recovers $F = ma$. The power of this method is that for complicated systems, writing energy is often far easier than resolving all forces.

**Key result:** If a system has total energy of the form:

$$E = \frac{1}{2}Ax^2 + \frac{1}{2}Bv^2$$

then it undergoes simple harmonic oscillation with angular frequency:

$$\boxed{\omega = \sqrt{\frac{A}{B}}}$$
