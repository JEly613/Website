## Angular Quantities Are the Same About Any Pivot

Consider a rigid body rotating with angular velocity $\omega$ about an arbitrary point 1. A second point 2, at distance $r$ from point 1, has speed $v = r\omega$.

Transforming into the reference frame of point 2, the angular velocity about point 2 is:

$$\omega_2 = \frac{v}{r} = \frac{r\omega}{r} = \omega$$

The angular velocity about any point on a rigid body is the same. This extends to angular acceleration as well â€” a useful fact since the torque equation $\tau = I\alpha$ can be applied about any convenient fixed point. â‹†

## Kinetic Energy of Rigid Bodies (Rotational KE)

The rotational kinetic energy of a rigid body is:

$$\boxed{K_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2}$$

**Derivation:** Using the result from the previous chapter, the total kinetic energy of any system is:

$$K_{\text{total}} = \frac{1}{2}Mv_{\text{cm}}^2 + \sum_i \frac{1}{2}m_i v_{i,\text{cm}}^2$$

For a rigid body rotating in the center-of-mass frame, each particle moves purely tangentially. Using polar coordinates centered at the center of mass:

$$\mathbf{u} = \frac{d\mathbf{r}_{i,\text{cm}}}{dt} + r_{i,\text{cm}}\frac{d\theta}{dt}$$

Since the body is rigid, $\frac{d r_{i,\text{cm}}}{dt} = 0$, so each particle's speed relative to the center of mass is $r_{i,\text{cm}}\omega$. Substituting:

$$K_{\text{total}} = \frac{1}{2}Mv_{\text{cm}}^2 + \sum_i \frac{1}{2}m_i r_{i,\text{cm}}^2\omega^2$$

Defining the **moment of inertia** about the center of mass $I_{\text{cm}} \equiv \sum_i m_i r_{i,\text{cm}}^2$:

$$\boxed{K_{\text{total}} = \frac{1}{2}Mv_{\text{cm}}^2 + \frac{1}{2}I_{\text{cm}}\omega^2}$$

For a rigid body rotating about a **fixed pivot** $i$, using the Parallel Axis Theorem ($I_i = I_{\text{cm}} + Mr_{i,\text{cm}}^2$) and noting that $v_{\text{cm}} = r_{i,\text{cm}}\omega$:

$$K_{\text{total}} = \frac{1}{2}Mv_{\text{cm}}^2 + \frac{1}{2}(I_i - Mr_{i,\text{cm}}^2)\omega^2 = \boxed{\frac{1}{2}I_i\omega^2}$$

## Parallel Axis Theorem

The **Parallel Axis Theorem** relates the moment of inertia about an arbitrary axis to the moment of inertia about a parallel axis through the center of mass.

Consider a rigid body with $I_{\text{cm}}$ about its center of mass, and an axis through point $O$ a distance $r_{\text{cm}}$ away. Writing $\mathbf{r}_i = \mathbf{r}_{i,\text{cm}} + \mathbf{r}_{\text{cm}}$:

$$I_O = \sum_i m_i r_i^2 = \sum_i m_i r_{i,\text{cm}}^2 + \sum_i m_i r_{\text{cm}}^2 + 2\mathbf{r}_{\text{cm}} \cdot \sum_i m_i \mathbf{r}_{i,\text{cm}}$$

The first term is $I_{\text{cm}}$, the second is $Mr_{\text{cm}}^2$, and the third is zero by definition of the center of mass. Thus:

$$\boxed{I_O = I_{\text{cm}} + Mr_{\text{cm}}^2}$$

## Common Moments of Inertia

All moments of inertia below are about an axis through the center of mass, using the integral definition $I = \int r^2\, dm$.

### Ring (mass $M$, radius $R$)

A small element of the ring has mass $dm = \frac{M}{2\pi}d\theta$, all at distance $R$:

$$I = \int_0^{2\pi} R^2 \cdot \frac{M}{2\pi}\,d\theta = \boxed{MR^2}$$

### Disk (mass $M$, radius $R$)

A small ring of width $dr$ has mass $dm = \frac{2M}{R^2}r\,dr$:

$$I = \int_0^R r^2 \cdot \frac{2M}{R^2}r\,dr = \frac{2M}{R^2}\cdot\frac{R^4}{4} = \boxed{\frac{1}{2}MR^2}$$

### Rod (mass $M$, length $L$, centered at origin)

$$I = \int_{-L/2}^{L/2} \frac{M}{L}x^2\,dx = \boxed{\frac{1}{12}ML^2}$$

### Spherical Shell (mass $M$, radius $R$)

Slicing the shell into rings at angle $\theta$ from the vertical, each ring has $dm = \frac{1}{2}M\sin\theta\,d\theta$ and radius $R\sin\theta$:

$$I = \int_0^{\pi} \frac{1}{2}MR^2\sin^3\theta\,d\theta = \frac{1}{2}MR^2\int_0^\pi \sin\theta(1-\cos^2\theta)\,d\theta = \boxed{\frac{2}{3}MR^2}$$

### Solid Sphere (mass $M$, radius $R$)

Integrating over spherical shells of radius $r$ and width $dr$, with $dm = \frac{3M}{R^3}r^2\,dr$:

$$I = \int_0^R \frac{2}{3}r^2\cdot\frac{3M}{R^3}r^2\,dr = \frac{2M}{R^3}\int_0^R r^4\,dr = \boxed{\frac{2}{5}MR^2}$$

## Perpendicular Axis Theorem

For a flat (2D) object lying in the $xy$-plane, the moment of inertia about the $z$-axis is:

$$I_z = \int (x^2 + y^2)\,dm = \int y^2\,dm + \int x^2\,dm$$

Since $\int y^2\,dm = I_x$ (distance from the $x$-axis) and $\int x^2\,dm = I_y$:

$$\boxed{I_z = I_x + I_y}$$

This is the **Perpendicular Axis Theorem**: for a planar object, the moment of inertia about an axis perpendicular to the plane equals the sum of the moments of inertia about two perpendicular axes in the plane. â‹†
